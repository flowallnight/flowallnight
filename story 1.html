Hook · Deliver Results

Cut SaaS churn 10% → 6% in 6 months by reframing the real problem from features to onboarding — pre–Series B, while juggling 2 other workstreams

Situation

Context & background

Petro.ai — venture-backed SaaS, ~45 ppl, Houston. Subsurface analytics for upstream oil & gas operators (Permian, Eagle Ford). Petroleum engineers + geoscientists used it to identify drilling opportunities and analyze well performance. Annual contracts $80K–$400K, 35 enterprise customers, ~60 contracts.

My role

Corporate Strategy & Insights Manager reporting to CEO Troy. Sat between product and commercial — owned analytical work behind product priorities, GTM strategy, and ops decisions. Key stakeholders: Robert (VP Product) and Amanda (CS lead), both reporting to the CEO.

The trigger

Mid-2021: SaaS churn climbed to 10% annually. Board pushed back hard at Q2 review. CEO Troy committed to bringing it to 7% within 6 months ahead of Series B. He asked me to own the customer feedback integration program.

Task

What I was trying to achieve

Build the mechanism to translate qualitative input from 60+ customers/prospects into prioritized product requirements feeding Robert's quarterly planning. Deadline: 3 months to first measurable churn impact. Personal accountability for hitting 7%.

Why it mattered

Direct board commitment pre–Series B. Without it, we'd walk into the raise with churn still trending up, and Robert's roadmap would keep chasing the wrong problem.

Risks if nothing happened

Series B terms deteriorate → compressed 2022 runway and hiring plan. Customer trust continues to erode. Robert's quarterly planning keeps prioritizing features over the actual driver of churn.

Additional context

The original design was clean — collect feedback from sales calls, support tickets, QBRs; synthesize themes; rank by frequency + revenue impact; hand to product. I scoped it as ~4 weeks of build with the rest for adoption and measurement. Four unanticipated obstacles surfaced that nearly killed it.

Action

Obstacle 1 → Action: Prioritized ruthlessly across 3 concurrent workstreams

Obstacle: When Troy handed me the feedback program, I was already running 2 major workstreams — the ops dashboard (in production, weekly maintenance, sales team waiting on a pending integration) and monthly KPI cadence (rolling out to 6 departments, 2 waiting on onboarding). Adding a third workstream with a board-level deadline meant I had to defer work I'd already promised, and defend those deferrals to stakeholders who weren't happy about it.

Action: Re-prioritized every Monday on business impact + reversibility. Feedback program consistently won — largest revenue impact + irreversible downside (board commitment, Series B). Dashboard and KPI cadence had lower weekly volatility and more forgiving timelines, so they could absorb deferral. Time-boxed each workstream — if any one consumed more than its allocated share, I'd burn through capacity.

Obstacle 2 → Action: Disconfirmed the stated problem with cohort data (weeks 2–4)

Obstacle: The stated customer problem (feature requests) wasn't the actual churn driver. Customers asked for visualization, data sources, custom reporting — but cross-referencing against the 12 churned accounts from prior 36 months showed they'd all failed onboarding within 60 days. Onboarding wasn't captured anywhere in existing feedback channels.

Action: Pulled product usage logs for every account, 36 months. Cohort analysis: 73% of churned had <4 sessions in first 30 days vs 12 sessions for retained. Sample size of 12 wasn't statistically robust on its own, so I ran the pattern forward-looking on current base — flagged 9 at-risk accounts on the same trajectory.

Obstacle 3 → Action: Reset Robert in a 1:1, not a group (week 5)

Obstacle: Robert pushed back in our biweekly group: "onboarding isn't a product problem, it's a CS problem." Reasonable disagreement — historically true at Petro.ai, and he had a quarter of committed roadmap items he didn't want to displace. Not dismissal, but a real conflict with a board deadline ticking.

Action: Rescheduled as 1:1 — group setting made him defensive. Framed as "help me disconfirm this" not "I'm right." Walked him through the cohort + 9 at-risk accounts. Asked: "If we don't fix onboarding, what happens to these 9 in the next 90 days?" His answer was honest — he didn't know, but the data was hard to argue with.

Obstacle 4 → Action: Proposed 80/20 split with kill criteria (week 6)

Obstacle: Even with Robert's agreement on the data, the roadmap couldn't absorb a full pivot — engineering capacity was already committed to feature backlog, and a winner-take-all framing would have killed the program.

Action: Proposed a structured split, not winner-take-all. Feature roadmap stays at 80% eng capacity; carve out 20% (~1 engineer) for onboarding fixes for one quarter as a test. Pre-committed kill criteria: if 60-day retention didn't improve 50% in 90 days, reallocate back. Let Robert say yes without betting his whole roadmap on my hypothesis.

Action 5 — Built rollout discipline (weeks 6+)

With Robert aligned and the engineering split in place, I owned the rollout end-to-end. Biweekly working sessions with Robert & Amanda. Friday one-page written status to Troy/Robert/Amanda: cohort metrics, decisions needed, risks, backlog changes. Forced weekly thinking, surfaced slip risk early, created a written record across competing priorities — the main mechanism that kept the program on track.

Action 6 — Built early-warning system + caught first save (week 7)

Built an early-warning system tracking the 9 at-risk accounts' session metrics weekly. Caught its first save in week 7: one account dropped 6 → 1 sessions/week over 2 weeks — exact divergence pattern from the churned cohort. Flagged Amanda Tuesday, ran 90-min workflow session Thursday with their 2 power users. Set up real workflow live using their data; Amanda scheduled 30-day check-in before we left the call. Renewed $180K/2yr 6 weeks later — prior pattern in similar accounts was non-renewal.

Result

Results achieved

Measured against three metrics: annualized churn (vs 7% target), 60-day retention on new cohort (vs 50% kill criteria), and retention on the 9 at-risk accounts. Churn 10% → 6% in 6 months — beat the 7% target. 2.5x 60-day retention on the new cohort in 90 days (5x the kill threshold). 7 of 9 at-risk accounts retained over the next 6 months (~$1.4M ARR); 2 churned for reasons outside our control (one acquired, one cut software budget firm-wide). Exited 2021 with healthier renewal book into Series B.

Long-term results

20% eng allocation for onboarding became a permanent line in the roadmap through my remaining tenure. Structured backlog became how product prioritized. At-risk early-warning became a recurring CS mechanism. Robert started looping me into prioritization pre-planning the next quarter — relationship outlasted the project.

Trade-offs (quality, cost, time)

Time: ops dashboard dropped to maintenance-only for a full quarter — no new features or integrations. Scope: KPI onboarding paused for 2 months; 1 of 6 departments escalated to Troy. Judgment: feedback program had the irreversible downside (board, fundraising, year-end deadline); the other two could absorb deferral. Not a free call — I had to defend the trade-off to two stakeholder groups.

What I learned / would do differently

Two things, both about sequencing. (1) Bring data to Robert in the first conversation, not after pushback — would've shortened the cycle ~2 weeks. (2) Build the early-warning system into the program design from week 1, not week 7 — we caught one account once the system was running, but 1–2 of the early-quarter churns we lost might've been caught if I'd built it from the start.

Watch-outs · Likely follow-ups

"Sample size of 12 isn't statistically robust" → Exactly why I ran the forward-looking analysis on 9 current at-risk accounts in parallel.

"How do you know the program caused the churn drop?" → Can't run a controlled trial on 35 customers. What I can say: 7 retained accounts all showed early-decline pre-intervention and reversed post; 2 churned for reasons we couldn't have addressed.

"How did the deferred work stakeholders react?" → One department escalated to Troy; I documented prioritization logic each Monday and brought it to him proactively before it became a problem.

"What changed about your relationship with Robert?" → He told me later what shifted his thinking was that I brought data to a 1:1 instead of pushing in the group. By next quarter he was looping me into prioritization pre-planning.
